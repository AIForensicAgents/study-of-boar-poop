# study-of-boar-poop
A field-friendly, educational study of boar scat (droppings, feces) for naturalists, students, and wildlife biologists — serious science with a light step.

---

## Overview
This repository collects observational notes, photographic examples, and interpretive guidance for identifying and recording wild boar (Sus scrofa and related feral pigs) scat in the field. It is designed as an educational resource for:

- Field naturalists and students learning scat identification
- Citizen science contributors documenting wildlife sign
- Conservationists and managers interested in non-invasive population and diet indicators

Content focuses on morphology, habitat & diet clues, and safe, ethical field practices. This is not a diagnostic lab manual — follow institutional protocols for any pathogen work.

---

## Why it matters
Scat is a compact, information-rich sample. When responsibly observed and documented, boar droppings can reveal:

- Presence/absence and relative abundance of wild boar in an area
- Diet composition and seasonal foraging shifts (acorns, roots, crops, invertebrates)
- Habitat use and movement patterns (latrines, trails, wallows)
- Evidence useful to management decisions (crop damage mitigation, restoration planning)

Non-invasive scat observation complements camera traps, tracking, and direct counts — especially where animals are secretive or nocturnal.

---

## Morphology notes
Practical, field-forward characteristics to record and compare:

- Shape & form
  - Typically tubular to clumped, often variable in size depending on diet and age.
  - Fresh, semi-formed droppings are softer and glossier; older droppings dry, fragment, and bleach.
- Size & scale
  - Diameter and length can vary: note approximate measurements (e.g., small, medium, large) and include a scale object or ruler in photos.
- Color & consistency
  - Color ranges from dark brown/black (fresh, high-protein content) to tan/grey (older or high-starch diets).
  - Consistency reflects moisture and diet: seed- and root-rich diets produce coarse, fibrous scat; insect or meat consumption can be softer.
- Content & inclusions
  - Visible seeds, nut fragments (acorns), plant fibers, insect exoskeletons, hair, and occasional bone fragments.
  - Presence of undigested grain or crop residues can indicate agricultural foraging.
- Contextual notes
  - Record freshness (within hours, days), associated tracks, proximity to rooting/wallowing, and nearby vegetation.

These morphological cues are observational — for definitive dietary or pathogen analysis, partner with appropriate labs.

## Habitat / Diet clues
Interpreting scat in its landscape context:

- Acorn- and mast-heavy contents
  - Indicate forested or oak-dominated habitats; important in temperate seasonal diets.
- Crop residues and domestic grains
  - Suggest edge habitat, agricultural fields, or human-associated foraging.
- Root/stem fibers and tuber fragments
  - Point to rooting behavior in moist soils, meadows, or wetland edges.
- Invertebrate fragments, earthworms
  - More common in wet seasons or invertebrate-rich soils; may indicate opportunistic feeding.
- Spatial patterns
  - Repeated deposits (latrines) along trails or territory edges can signal preferred pathways or social marking behavior.

Always pair scat interpretation with habitat descriptions, GPS location, and other field signs (tracks, wallows, rooting).

## Ethics & safety
Professional and ethical field work is non-negotiable. Key principles:

- Respect wildlife and property
  - Do not harass animals or damage habitat to obtain samples. Obtain landowner or agency permission before entering private or managed lands.
- Personal safety & biosecurity
  - Avoid direct contact with feces. Use disposable gloves and tools (e.g., trowel) if handling is necessary for photography or collection, and wash hands thoroughly afterward.
  - Do not collect or transport scat samples for laboratory analysis without institutional approval, permits, and biohazard containment measures.
- Public health considerations
  - Boars can carry zoonotic agents. If you suspect an infected animal or find carcasses, notify appropriate wildlife or public health authorities.
- Data ethics
  - When sharing location data for rare species or on sensitive lands, consider data obfuscation to prevent disturbance or exploitation.
- Responsible reporting
  - Cite uncertainty in observations. Scat can be confounded with other large mammals (e.g., domestic pigs, deer in some conditions); use multiple lines of evidence.

If your project requires pathogen testing, genetic analysis, or invasive sampling, partner with accredited labs and follow local/regional regulatory frameworks.
